Competitive Research

Six contractor / SMB products that offer both proposal/contract creation AND payment processing. Same panel is used to answer all questions, so comparisons are apples-to-apples.

Q1How do competitors deliver contract and payment info to users?

Combined view of file structure (separate vs combined) and download UX (where and how users get them). These two questions overlap heavily — answering them together.

Appending payment data to a signed contract has no precedent in this competitor set. The team's legal concern is well-founded. The pattern across the panel is to keep the signed artifact immutable and provide payment data as a separate report — or, in HoneyBook's case, as a client-portal experience that isn't a mutating PDF.
Two PDFs, one click. When the user selects Proposal & Payment Statement in the dropdown, the browser downloads two distinct files at once:
  • Proposal PDF — the original signed contract (immutable, byte-identical to what was signed)
  • Payment Statement PDF — a separate document containing the Payment Summary, Invoice Schedule, and Payment Records
Files stay legally distinct (matches 5/6 competitors who never mutate the signed contract). UX stays one-click for users who want both — addressing the customer feedback that triggered this design.

Q2What do payment + invoice displays look like?

Visual building blocks used in the screens and PDFs of these 6 — plus how each handles bundling invoice and payment history in a single export.

All 6 competitors use the same visual taxonomy.
  • Status colors: green = paid, red = overdue, orange / yellow = partial or pending, gray = unsent / no status — universal across all 6.
  • Progress display takes one of three forms: % bar (Jobber, HoneyBook), aging buckets (QuickBooks), or schedule of values (JobTread, Buildertrend).
  • Transaction display is always tabular — Date / Method / Amount columns are standard.
